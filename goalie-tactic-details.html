As a software member of Thunderbots, UBCâ€™s autonomous robot soccer design team, a major project I took on was implementing a portion of the gameplay logic, the goalie tactic, as a state-machine. In C++, I developed the states, events, and transitions of the state-machine, which defined behaviour for blocking and intercepting, clearing, and tracking the ball. Furthermore, I designed simulated integration tests that ran the software through a sequence of events, using validation functions to verify that the robot performed the correct state transition and associated actions. The purpose of implementing gameplay as a state-machine was to make our code more modular, templatized, and scalable, which allowed for quicker debugging and accommodated further development in the future by other team members.

States:
Find best position: the goalie positions itself between the ball and the goal
Block ball: the goalie moves to intercept, and automatically clears the ball on interception
Clear ball: the goalie intercepts the ball, then dribbles to an appropriate location to clear it

Events:
panic: ball is moving faster than the panic speed threshold, and toward the goal
safe: ball is outside of the friendly defence area, and not moving above the panic threshold
caution: ball is inside the friendly defence area but not moving above the panic threshold
